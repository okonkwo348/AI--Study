1. Write a prompt asking the same question (e.g., "What is climate change?").

    That is a **question-style prompt** because it’s **asking for information** rather than instructing an action.

* **Starts with a question word:** “What” → asking for explanation or definition.
* **Goal:** You want the AI (or a person) to provide an answer or explanation.
* **Not a command:** It doesn’t tell the AI to “Explain” or “List,” it simply **asks for information**.

**Example comparison:**

| Prompt                                    | Type               | Explanation                                       |
| ----------------------------------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------- |
| “What is climate change?”                 | Question-style     | Asking for a definition or explanation            |
| “Explain climate change in simple terms.” | Direct instruction | Tells the AI to perform an action (explain)       |
| “Climate change causes and effects.”      | Statement-style    | Just mentions a topic, no question or instruction |

        From a scientific perspective, **climate change** refers to long-term alterations in the statistical properties of the Earth’s climate system, including temperature, precipitation patterns, atmospheric circulation, and frequency of extreme weather events, over decades to millennia.

        The primary driver of contemporary climate change is the enhanced **greenhouse effect** caused by increased concentrations of anthropogenic greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O). These gases absorb and re-emit outgoing longwave infrared radiation, leading to a positive radiative forcing and a net increase in the Earth’s energy balance.

        A major contributor to rising greenhouse gas concentrations is the combustion of fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial processes since the pre-industrial era. This has resulted in a measurable increase in global mean surface temperature, commonly referred to as global warming, which is a key component of broader climate change.

        Climate change also involves complex feedback mechanisms within the Earth system. For example, the ice-albedo feedback reduces surface reflectivity as polar ice melts, increasing solar absorption, while water vapor feedback amplifies warming because warmer air holds more moisture, itself a potent greenhouse gas.

        Additionally, climate change impacts multiple interconnected subsystems, including the cryosphere (glacier and ice sheet loss), hydrosphere (sea-level rise due to thermal expansion and ice melt), biosphere (ecosystem shifts and biodiversity loss), and atmosphere (changes in circulation patterns such as jet streams and monsoons).

        Scientific evidence for climate change is derived from multiple empirical data sources, including paleoclimate proxies (ice cores, tree rings, sediment records), satellite observations, instrumental temperature records, and climate models that simulate Earth system dynamics under varying forcing scenarios.

        OUTPUT:
        Climate change is the long-term change in the Earth’s weather patterns and average temperatures, mainly caused by human activities.

        In simple terms, it means the planet is getting warmer because of gases released into the atmosphere from things like burning fuel (cars, factories, electricity) and cutting down forests. These gases trap heat from the sun, acting like a blanket around the Earth. This is called the greenhouse effect.

        As the Earth warms, we start to see real-world impacts. Temperatures rise, heatwaves become more frequent, and weather patterns become less predictable. Some areas may experience stronger storms, heavier rainfall, or longer droughts.

        Climate change also affects the environment. Glaciers and ice caps are melting, sea levels are rising, and ecosystems are changing as animals and plants struggle to adapt to new conditions.

        For everyday people, climate change can influence food supply, water availability, health, and the cost of living. For example, extreme weather can damage crops, cause flooding, or disrupt infrastructure.

        In short, climate change is not just about hotter weather—it is about long-term changes to the planet’s climate system that affect nature, economies, and people’s daily lives around the world.

From this viewpoint, they question how much of current warming is caused specifically by human actions versus natural variability.

        A skeptical perspective also looks at climate models and projections. While scientists use complex models to predict future climate patterns, critics point out that models rely on assumptions and may contain uncertainties, especially when forecasting long-term impacts decades into the future.

        Regarding effects and impacts, a critic might acknowledge observable changes such as rising temperatures, shifting weather patterns, and melting ice, but question the scale, speed, or attribution of these changes. They may ask whether extreme weather events are directly caused by climate change or part of natural climate fluctuations.

They may argue that climate policies—such as emission regulations or energy transitions—can have significant costs for industries, developing economies, and everyday consumers, and therefore should be carefully evaluated against the projected risks.

        In essence, a skeptical and critical lens does not necessarily deny climate change exists, but instead emphasizes uncertainty, the role of natural factors, the limitations of predictions, and the need for balanced discussion about scientific evidence, impacts, and policy responses.
